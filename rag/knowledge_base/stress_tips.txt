[HIGH]
High stress levels are associated with serious health risks including coronary heart disease, hypertension, autoimmune diseases, obesity, migraines, and stroke. Chronic work-related stress increases risk of coronary heart disease by 1.4 times and is a major predictor of major depressive disorder and metabolic syndrome.
Immediately reduce overtime hours and establish firm boundaries between work and personal time. Avoid checking work messages outside of working hours.
Practice Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) techniques such as body scan, breath-awareness meditation, and gentle yoga. Even short daily sessions of 10-20 minutes have measurable effects on stress reduction.
Seek professional support or a structured stress management programme. Cognitive-behavioural interventions have shown medium to large reductions in stress (effect sizes d=0.44 to 1.70) and are the most evidence-supported approach.
High stress is linked to low physical activity, poor sleep, overeating, and social isolation — address these together rather than in isolation.
If you are a healthcare professional, be aware that burnout prevalence can reach 75% in medical settings and is an independent predictor of medical errors. Prioritise self-care as a professional responsibility.

[MODERATE]
Moderate stress indicates manageable but building pressure that should not be ignored. Left unaddressed, moderate stress can escalate to burnout, which involves emotional exhaustion, depersonalisation, and reduced personal accomplishment.
Improve sleep quality by establishing a consistent sleep schedule. Mindfulness-based interventions have been shown to significantly improve sleep quality scores compared to non-mindfulness stress management approaches.
Begin a mindfulness practice. Research meta-analyses show that MBSR produces moderate effects on anxiety (Hedge's g=0.55), depression (g=0.68), and overall distress even in non-clinical, healthy populations.
Organise tasks using priority frameworks and delegate where possible to reduce cognitive load and time pressure.
Engage in regular physical exercise. Chronic stress is directly associated with low physical activity, and exercise is a natural stress buffer that supports upward spiralling of positive well-being.
Practice early detection of stress markers — notice physical sensations, mood changes, and thought patterns as early warning signs. Earlier detection creates more opportunity to choose a different response before stress escalates.
Incorporate self-compassion practices. Research shows that changes in compassion measures correlate strongly with improvements in clinical stress and anxiety outcomes.

[LOW]
Low stress levels suggest a healthy work-life balance. The goal at this stage is maintenance, resilience-building, and prevention.
Maintain consistent daily routines including regular sleep, meals, physical activity, and social connection. Consistency reinforces psychological stability.
Continue engaging in wellness activities such as mindfulness meditation, exercise, and hobbies. Mindfulness gains tend to increase over time — follow-up studies show effects are maintained and even grow stronger at 19 weeks post-intervention.
Practice legitimising self-care — actively schedule time for personal well-being and treat it as non-negotiable, just as you would a work commitment.
Build a personal stress-management toolkit so you are prepared when pressure increases. Knowing you have coping strategies available is itself a psychological asset that supports resilience.
Cultivate an upward spiral of positive well-being by intentionally noticing and savouring positive moments during your day. This broadens attention to good experiences and further reduces the burden of any residual stress.
Maintain healthy boundaries and communicate your limits clearly to colleagues and managers. Sustainable high performance depends on protecting personal energy and recovery time.
